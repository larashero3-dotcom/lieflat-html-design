Market Signal Intelligence › Gold report, April 2025

Gold broke through $3,000, $3,200, $3,300, $3,400 and $3,500 in a single month; each move triggered by a distinct policy shock

Spot gold price (XAU/USD), Jan–Apr 2025
$ per troy ounce     ━ XAU/USD

Gold prices hit multiple record highs in April 2025, surging past $3,500/oz for the first time. The rally accelerated after US-China tariffs reached an effective 145% rate, the highest bilateral tariff since the Smoot-Hawley era.

The IMF cut its 2025 US growth forecast from 2.7% to 1.8%, citing tariff uncertainty. Global growth was downgraded to 2.8% from 3.3%. Consumer confidence deteriorated across all major economies.

President Trump's public attacks on Fed Chair Powell raised questions about central bank independence, triggering a flight from US assets. The dollar fell to a 3-year low, making gold more attractive for non-USD buyers.

Gold ETFs saw inflows of 226.5 tonnes ($21.1B) in Q1 2025 alone — the largest quarterly figure since Q1 2022 when Russia invaded Ukraine. Central banks purchased 333 tonnes in Q4 2024, up 54% year-on-year.

IMF slashed growth forecasts across all major economies; US saw the steepest downgrade at -0.9 percentage points

IMF Real GDP growth forecast, 2025
Percent, year-over-year    ■ Apr-25   ■ Jan-25

The IMF's April 2025 World Economic Outlook reflects the sharpest single-quarter downgrade since the COVID-19 pandemic. The US growth outlook was cut by 0.9 percentage points, driven entirely by tariff uncertainty.

China's forecast was reduced by 0.5pp to 4.0%, reflecting both reduced export demand and the retaliatory tariff escalation. The Eurozone saw a smaller cut (-0.2pp) as it benefits from some trade diversion.

The simultaneous downgrade across all regions amplified gold demand: when growth expectations fall everywhere, there's no "rotation" trade available. Capital flows to the only asset that benefits from universal uncertainty.

Fed Chair Powell stated in mid-April that the US economy "appears to be slowing," with consumer spending growing only modestly. This language shift made rate cut expectations more concrete, further supporting gold.

Gold and the dollar moved in opposite directions at historic velocity; the negative correlation reached -0.92 in April

Gold price vs US Dollar Index (DXY), indexed Jan 1 = 100
━ Gold (XAU)    ━ DXY    ━ S&P 500

The dollar's decline to a 3-year low has been the most direct accelerant for gold in April. A weaker dollar makes gold cheaper for non-USD buyers, who represent ~70% of global physical demand.

The correlation turned sharply negative after Trump's April 21 attack on Fed Chair Powell. Markets interpreted the remarks as a signal that monetary policy independence may be compromised, triggering capital flight from US assets across equities, bonds, and the dollar simultaneously.

The S&P 500's -12% YTD performance marks the widest divergence from gold since the 2008 financial crisis. This pattern is consistent with a regime change: investors are rotating from "growth" to "preservation" mode.

Independent market analyst: "Gold is recalibrating to reflect epic changes in the global financial system — a fundamental shift in confidence in the world's reserve currency."

Central bank purchases surged to 333 tonnes in Q4 2024, up 54% from the same quarter in 2023. This acceleration coincided with Trump's election win, as central banks anticipated policy volatility.

China's PBOC added gold for 5 consecutive months through March 2025. Reports indicate China is considering overseas warehouses for international gold settlement on the Shanghai Gold Exchange.

A bullion-platform chief analyst: "Central bank demand is very likely chasing gold's move higher, because the rising tide of policy uncertainty only hardens gold's appeal as a geopolitical asset."

Goldman Sachs raised its year-end forecast to $3,700 (base) and $3,810 (if central bank buying sustains at 100t/month). ANZ targets $3,600.
